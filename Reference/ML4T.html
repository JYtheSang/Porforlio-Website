01 — Overview

What this project is

This is a semester-long project for Georgia Tech's CS 7646 – Machine Learning for Trading, one of the most data-intensive courses in the OMSA program. The goal: build a complete algorithmic trading system that uses machine learning to make buy/sell decisions for JPMorgan (JPM) stock — without any pre-built ML libraries for the learning algorithms themselves.

The system spans three interconnected layers: a custom ML engine (decision trees, random forests), a technical indicator library, and two trading strategies — one hand-crafted from market intuition, one learned from data. Both were benchmarked against a simple buy-and-hold portfolio over the 2008–2009 financial crisis (in-sample) and 2010–2011 (out-of-sample).

Why it matters for engineering hiring managers: Every algorithm — decision tree construction, bagging, indicator math — was implemented from scratch in pure NumPy/pandas. No sklearn. No shortcuts.
02 — Technical Approach

Three-phase pipeline

01

Custom Machine Learning Engine

Built decision tree learners from scratch using only NumPy arrays — no scikit-learn or other ML libraries.

  • DTLearner: Splits on the feature with highest absolute Pearson correlation to the target. Tree stored as a flat NumPy array for cache efficiency.
  • RTLearner: Splits on a randomly selected feature — faster to train, reduces variance at the cost of slight bias.
  • BagLearner: Bootstrap aggregation wrapper; trains n base learners on resampled data and averages predictions. Configured with 150 RT bags.
  • Experiments: Measured RMSE and R² vs. leaf size to characterize overfitting. Confirmed bagging reduces overfitting: in-sample RMSE rose while out-of-sample RMSE held stable.
02

Technical Indicator Library

Implemented five indicators from scratch, each tuned empirically against JPM price data to surface meaningful signal without over-fitting to noise.

Bollinger Band %

Measures price relative to a 20-day rolling mean ± 1.5σ. Values near 0 signal oversold; near 1 signal overbought.

Price / SMA Ratio

Normalizes price against its 20-day simple moving average. Ratio < 0.96 suggests mean-reversion long opportunity.

Momentum

15-day percentage price change. Captures trend direction without lag from smoothing.

PPO (Percentage Price Oscillator)

EMA(8) − EMA(26) / EMA(26), plus a signal line EMA(9). Crossovers used to detect trend shifts.

Stochastic %K/%D

10-period stochastic with 3-period smoothing. Used during exploratory analysis; PPO crossover ultimately provided cleaner signal.

03

Dual Trading Strategies

Two strategies were built and compared head-to-head — one rule-based, one ML-driven.

  • Manual Strategy: A weighted voting system across all four indicators. Bollinger Band % carries 1.5× weight; SMA and Momentum carry 1×; PPO crossover carries 0.5×. A net signal ≥ 2 triggers a long position (1,000 shares); ≤ −2 triggers short. Thresholds were hand-tuned through iterative backtesting on the training period.
  • Strategy Learner: A BagLearner (150 RTLearner bags, leaf_size=5) trained on the four indicator features. Labels encode 5-day forward returns: >2.5% + impact → long (+1), <−2.5% − impact under normal volatility → short (−1), else hold (0). Market impact and commission costs ($9.95/trade) are factored into both training labels and portfolio simulation.

Experiment 1 — Strategy Learner vs Manual Strategy vs Benchmark

In-sample, all three strategies were compared. The Strategy Learner, having been trained on the same period, generally matched or exceeded the Manual Strategy. The out-of-sample comparison tests real generalization — both handcrafted and ML strategies defended against the benchmark's 8.5% loss.

Experiment 2 — Market Impact Sensitivity

A key question in algorithmic trading: how does market impact (slippage) affect strategy viability? The Strategy Learner was re-run across impact values from 0.000 to 0.050. As impact increases, the learner correctly adapts — trading less frequently and targeting larger moves to remain profitable after friction costs.

Theoretical Upper Bound

As a reference ceiling, a Theoretically Optimal Strategy (ToC) was computed using perfect future knowledge — buying every day before an up-move, shorting every day before a down-move. This is physically impossible to trade but establishes the maximum extractable value from the price series.

04 — Key Decisions

What I chose and why

Random Trees over Decision Trees for the base learner

DTLearner selects the best feature at each split (highest correlation), which makes it prone to overfitting on noisy financial data. RTLearner selects a random feature, injecting variance diversity across the 150-bag ensemble. Experiments confirmed that RT-based bagging produced better out-of-sample RMSE than DT-based bagging at equivalent leaf sizes.

Volatility-adjusted labels for short signals

Short positions were only labeled during normal volatility periods (rolling 5-day std dev ≤ 1.5× average). During high-volatility regimes (e.g., crisis spikes), short signals were suppressed — reducing whipsaw losses when the model would otherwise try to short into chaotic price action.

One-day prediction lag to prevent lookahead bias

Both strategies shift trade execution by one day: today's indicators predict tomorrow's trade. This is a strict data discipline requirement — any lookahead contamination would produce unrealistically good backtests that fail in live trading.

Weighted indicator voting in the Manual Strategy

Rather than requiring unanimous signals, a weighted scoring system (Bollinger Band 1.5×, SMA/Momentum 1×, PPO 0.5×) allows partial agreement to still trigger a trade. Threshold tuning showed that requiring ≥2 composite score meaningfully reduced false positives on the training set while keeping the strategy responsive.

Impact baked into training labels, not just simulation

The forward-return thresholds for labeling long/short include ± self.impact. This means the learner is never rewarded for trades that only appear profitable before slippage — aligning the learning objective directly with real-world execution costs.
